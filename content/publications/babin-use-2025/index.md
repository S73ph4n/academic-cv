abstract: 'Diagnosis of microbial bloodstream infections involves two key steps: detection
  and identification of the pathogen. In clinical laboratories, these two processes
  are clearly separated, with a culture step (detection) and a mass spectrometry step
  (identification). A few rare technologies enable coupling, but they are a priori
  (analysis with a specific probe such as an antibody) or even destructive (PCR),
  two features that make their implementation problematic. We propose here a revolutionary
  method for detecting and identifying bacterial contamination in a complex sample
  (such as human whole blood) without a priori, manipulation or addition of reagent.
  Blood culture bottles were instrumented and sterilized with polyaniline-based (pH
  sensitive conductive polymer) electrochemical sensors. Over a hundred human blood
  samples were tested, inoculated with reference bacterial strains. Thanks to potentiometric
  measurement during culture, we are able not only to detect bacterial growth (without
  false positives or false negatives), but also to extract an electrochemical signature
  specific to the microbial species detected.  We have coupled our instrumented bottle
  device with a machine learning algorithm to classify these signatures and thus recognize
  the pathogen directly during culture, without any human intervention. Furthermore,
  the detection times obtained by our method are comparable to those of current commercial
  optical transduction systems (Bactec™ and BacT/ALERT®). It appears that we detect
  more than just pH changes in PAni-contaminated blood solution. To better understand
  the nature of our signals, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy characterizations
  were carried out in an experimental set-up enabling real-time extraction of bacterial
  metabolites during culture. These results reveal the decisive role played by microbial
  growth on the electrode surface in creating the electrochemical signatures that
  ultimately enable identification. Finally, this work suggests that direct electron
  transfer by bacteria, coupled with the detection of pH variations in PAni, is the
  electrochemical mechanism responsible for species-dependent dynamics. This unique
  new method could significantly reduce the time and cost of diagnosing bloodstream
  infections. Clinical studies are currently underway to validate the device under
  real-life conditions.'
